Comprehensive Plan to Improve English Pronunciation, Grammar, and Interview Communication

Practical training for a 56-year-old American (Latino background) who wants better pronunciation, vocabulary, and clearer speaking—especially for job interviews.

Pronouns (she/he) Past tense -ed endings /dʒ/ sound (“manager”) Interview answers

What’s really going on (why improvement “stops” after many years)

After years of using English, many learners reach fossilization: the brain has learned a “good enough” version that works day-to-day, so errors stop correcting themselves automatically.

  • Improvement usually requires: more speaking time (not just listening), frequent feedback, deliberate pronunciation practice, and structured speaking practice.
  • It’s not about intelligence. It’s about building a consistent feedback loop and practicing the exact skills that are weak.
Key idea: The fastest progress comes from small daily drills + recording your voice + getting consistent correction on only 1–2 targets at a time.

1) She/he confusion: fix it with a simple speaking habit

This is common for Spanish speakers because Spanish often allows dropping the subject, and the brain may not “auto-check” gender in fast English speech.

Real-life strategy: Name-first, pronoun-second

  • If you’re not 100% sure, repeat the person’s name or role instead of guessing.
  • Example: “Maria said Maria will call later.”
  • Example: “My manager told me my manager wants the report.”
Safe modern option: Use singular they when gender is unknown or you want to avoid mistakes. Example: “My manager said they will email me.”

5-minute daily drill (record yourself)

  1. Pick 10 photos (family, celebrities, coworkers).
  2. Say 3 sentences each:
    • “This is ___.”
    • “She/He works as ___.”
    • “Today she/he is going to ___.”
  3. Record and replay. Listening catches errors your brain missed while speaking.

2) Past tense problems: make speaking easier (less thinking)

In conversation, the brain can’t reliably “compute grammar” fast. Use patterns that automatically produce correct tense.

Two safe past-tense patterns

Pattern A: Time marker + “I” + past verb

  • “Last week I worked late.”
  • “Yesterday I met my friend.”

Pattern B: “I did” + base verb (helps avoid irregular forms)

  • “I did talk to him.”
  • “I did go to the office.”
  • “I did wash the dishes.”

Interview time markers (practice these first)

  • “In my last job…”
  • “At my previous company…”
  • “Last year…”
  • “In the past, I used to…”
Tip: Starting with a past-time phrase often “locks” your brain into past tense for the rest of the sentence.

3) The “-ed” ending: it’s not “ed” most of the time

This is a mechanical rule with big payoff. English -ed has 3 pronunciations:

Rule 1: /t/ (after unvoiced sounds)

  • talked → “talkt”
  • washed → “washt”
  • stopped → “stopt”

Rule 2: /d/ (after voiced sounds)

  • played → “playd”
  • cleaned → “cleand”
  • called → “calld”

Rule 3: /ɪd/ (only after t or d)

  • wanted → “wantid”
  • needed → “needid”

8-minute drill (4×/week)

  1. Whisper endings: “t… t… t…” then “d… d… d…”
  2. Attach it:
    • talk → talkt
    • wash → washt
    • play → playd
    • clean → cleand
  3. Use in sentences (record):
    • “Yesterday I talked to my manager.”
    • “I washed the car.”
    • “I played soccer.”
If “talkt” is too hard: temporarily add a tiny vowel, then reduce it over time: talk-uh-t → talkt.

4) “manager” → “manayer”: train the /dʒ/ sound

“Manager” uses the /dʒ/ sound (like j in “job”). Many Spanish speakers replace it with a “y”-like sound.

Target pronunciation: manager ≈ /ˈmænɪdʒər/ (“MAN-ih-jer”)

How to make /dʒ/

  • Make “ch” (as in “church”), then add voice (throat vibration).
  • “ch” + voice = “j” (/dʒ/).

Minimal pairs (5 minutes/day)

  • yet vs jet
  • yam vs jam
  • yoke vs joke

Put it in job phrases

  • “My manager asked me…”
  • “I spoke with the manager.”

5) Job interviews: structure beats perfect grammar

Interviews reward clarity. A simple reusable structure helps her express ideas even with limited vocabulary.

Framework 1: PREP

  • Point: main idea
  • Reason: why
  • Example: proof story
  • Point again: connect to the job

PREP example:

  • Point: “I’m very reliable.”
  • Reason: “I plan my day and communicate early if something changes.”
  • Example: “In my last job, I covered shifts and met deadlines.”
  • Point: “So you can count on me to deliver consistently.”

Framework 2: STAR (best for behavioral questions)

  • Situation
  • Task
  • Action
  • Result
Best practice: Prepare 6–8 STAR stories (problem solved, conflict, mistake & learning, teamwork, pressure, learning something new, process improvement, customer help). Reuse them across many questions.

6) Fast improvement schedule (realistic, measurable)

Daily (20–25 minutes)

  1. 5 min: Pronoun photo drill (record it)
  2. 8 min: -ed drill (talked/washed/played/cleaned)
  3. 5 min: /dʒ/ drill (jet/jam/manager sentences)
  4. 5–7 min: One interview question using PREP (record)

2–3× per week (30–60 minutes)

  • Practice with a partner (friend/coworker/tutor) and correct only two things:
    • pronouns + past tense, or
    • pronouns + -ed, etc.
  • Too many corrections at once makes people shut down.

Weekly

  • One mock interview (30 minutes). Record audio.
  • Review and write down:
    • the 3 most common errors that week
    • 3 “upgrade phrases” to use next week

7) The right kind of feedback (how to break fossilized errors)

Consistent correction from someone trained to hear pronunciation patterns is one of the fastest ways to improve.

  • An accent reduction coach (even once per week)
  • A speech-language pathologist (SLP) who works with adult pronunciation
  • Community college ESL speaking classes
  • Toastmasters (confidence + structured speaking)
Note: Language exchange partners help only if they actively correct her (not just polite conversation).

8) Confidence + comparison (why peers improved more)

Comparing herself to others is understandable, but the difference is usually exposure + feedback + willingness to speak, not “talent.” If embarrassment causes her to speak less, avoid past tense, or avoid interviews, her English can stay stuck.

Break the loop: small daily practice + recordings + limited correction targets + structured interview answers. Noticeable improvement is common within 4–8 weeks when practice is consistent.
